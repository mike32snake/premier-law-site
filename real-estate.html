Florida Land Trusts

Land trusts are one of many tools in Premier Law's real estate toolkit. In a Florida land trust, only the trustee's name appears in public records, while beneficiaries retain rights to use the property, receive income, and transfer interests directly to heirs.

These trusts can be incorporated into purchase, sale, development, or lease transactions as part of a broader strategy to provide privacy, flexibility, and asset protection.

Real Estate FAQs

Florida does not require an attorney for lease drafting, but it is strongly recommended — especially for commercial leases or landlords with multiple units. A lease is a binding contract that can span years. An attorney ensures your lease complies with Florida statutes, protects your rights, and holds up in court if you ever need to enforce it.
The Florida eviction process begins with proper written notice to the tenant (typically 3 days for nonpayment, 7 days for lease violations). If the tenant does not comply, the landlord files an eviction complaint in county court. After service and a hearing, the court may issue a final judgment and writ of possession. Strict procedural compliance is required at every step — errors can restart the process. Premier Law handles evictions from notice through writ of possession.
Key commercial lease provisions include: base rent and rent escalation schedules, CAM (common area maintenance) charges and caps, tenant improvement allowances, exclusivity clauses, personal guaranty requirements, renewal and termination options, assignment and subletting rights, and default and cure periods. An attorney's review can identify landlord-favorable traps and negotiate better terms before you sign.
While Florida does not legally require an attorney at closing, having one is strongly recommended. A real estate attorney reviews contracts, identifies risks, ensures proper title transfer, and protects your interests throughout the transaction. For complex deals, commercial transactions, or properties with title issues, legal representation is essential.

In Florida, landlords may evict tenants for material lease violations (such as unauthorized occupants, property damage, or illegal activity), for failure to maintain the premises, or for remaining after the lease term has ended (holdover tenants). The specific notice requirements differ depending on the reason for eviction. An attorney ensures the right notice is used and the correct process is followed.
Surplus funds are the excess money remaining after a mortgage foreclosure sale or tax deed sale pays off the outstanding debt. If the property sells for more than what is owed, the former owner may be entitled to the difference. Recovery requires filing a claim with the appropriate court or agency within specific time limits.
A Florida land trust is a legal arrangement where real property is held by a trustee for the benefit of named beneficiaries. The key advantage is privacy — only the trustee's name appears in public records. Land trusts also simplify probate, provide a degree of asset protection, and make it easier to transfer property interests.
