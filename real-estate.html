Florida Land Trusts

Land trusts are one of many tools in Premier Law's real estate toolkit. In a Florida land trust, only the trustee's name appears in public records, while beneficiaries retain rights to use the property, receive income, and transfer interests directly to heirs.

These trusts can be incorporated into purchase, sale, development, or lease transactions as part of a broader strategy to provide privacy, flexibility, and asset protection.

Surplus Funds Recovery

When properties are sold through mortgage foreclosure or tax deed sales, there are sometimes surplus funds remaining after the outstanding debts are paid. These funds rightfully belong to the former property owner, but recovering them requires navigating a specific legal process.

Real Estate FAQs

While Florida does not legally require an attorney at closing, having one is strongly recommended. A real estate attorney reviews contracts, identifies risks, ensures proper title transfer, and protects your interests throughout the transaction. For complex deals, commercial transactions, or properties with title issues, legal representation is essential.
Attorneys Key Title is Premier Law's affiliated title company. They handle title searches, title insurance, escrow services, and closing coordination for residential and commercial real estate transactions throughout Florida. Working with both Premier Law and Attorneys Key Title provides clients with a seamless, end-to-end transaction experience.
Surplus funds are the excess money remaining after a mortgage foreclosure sale or tax deed sale pays off the outstanding debt. If the property sells for more than what is owed, the former owner may be entitled to the difference. Recovery requires filing a claim with the appropriate court or agency within specific time limits.
A Florida land trust is a legal arrangement where real property is held by a trustee for the benefit of named beneficiaries. The key advantage is privacy — only the trustee's name appears in public records. Land trusts also simplify probate, provide a degree of asset protection, and make it easier to transfer property interests.
Title insurance protects against losses from defects in the title that were not discovered during the title search. This can include undisclosed liens, forged documents, recording errors, or unknown heirs with claims to the property. In Florida, it's standard for buyers to purchase an owner's title insurance policy at closing.
Commercial transactions are typically more complex than residential deals.
